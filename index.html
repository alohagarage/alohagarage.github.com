The End of the End?

In a recent article for the MIT Technology Review, Editor in Chief Jason Pontin offers a good summary and explanation of the current state of the art of defeatism. He points to the now-commonplace plaint among venture capitalist about the paucity of big ideas: best summed up in my opinion, by Mike Arrington who decried the new explosion of "dipshit companies" (it's a social-local-mobile app for vegan mountain bikers!). Peter Thiel claims that all Twitter has given the world is job security for 500 people into the next decade. Whatever flavor of bitching you choose, it is obvious that the tide is against Solving Big Problems.

But before we complain too much, we should stop and give ourselves a pat on the back. The world and the lives of the humans on it is better now than it has ever been by almost any metric you choose.

The End of Technological Problems

Fear of Humanity

I have a hypothesis; all the more strident for the fact that it's unfalsifiable. My hypothesis is that WWII left most of the Western World frightened of the human will, particularly as it manifests itself en masse as the political will. We should not forget the extent to which the middle of this century was truly a triumph of the will. Take the (for some reason) less controversial example of Japan.

Japan was feudal until around 1868, at which point, we opened Japan by force. By 1931, the had invaded China a country heavily supported by the most powerful industrial powers in the world. To put this in perspective, the last vestiges of Feudalism in Western Europe were in the 15th century. Which means that, within 42 years, Japan was able to industrialize enough to threaten the West. This was not done through the "normal" course of technological evolution, but rather through an almost incomprehensible force of political will.

It is important to realize that the Axis powers (and Russia) truly shared this idea: That "natural" technological and economic advancement would doom their nations to always be the servants of countries that either had a head start or had natural geo-political advantages. They were underdeveloped and backward. They knew that if they were to let things ride, they would have been eaten alive. So they embraced a multitude of political ideologies that placed the will above technology, above the economy; of these, Italian Fascism was the most pure, since it did not need an admixture of Blood and Soil rascism to make it go.

And when we, the West, came face to face with this and the risks associated it, we flinched. Instead of trying to harness the possibilities of the political will, increasingly necessary in an age when technology and the market threaten to spin out of control, we simply ignored it. And we did what all cultures convinced of their own progress do: we made a virtue of a necessity. We decided that this awful power which we had shirked was actually bad and we were good by virtue of not having it. We began to treat our political impotence as itself a sign of our moral superiority

More amazing than this gambit which, given the atrocities of WWII, are somewhat understandable, is how long it has worked. I believe that this is because:

  1. Our economy was already crapping on everybody.
  2. The rest of the world was far more affected by WWII.
  3. Our country is geographically superior to most major powers.
And here is maybe my most controversial claim: technological problems are hard on a personal level, but easy on a societal level. Given enough money enough time and politians with a modicum of sense, these problems will be solved. The people who work hard on them every day should be proud, but society taking credit for it is like taking credit for because you didn't chop them down. Technological problems also allow us to skirt (if we want to) truly human problems like What is justice, What is the right way to live, What is love. The "Scientific" answers to these questions (which are abundant) are farcical and patently unsatisfying to anyone who is not getting paid to provide them. The fear of the human forced us to flee into the other extremes; into the depths of space and the sea, into the mysteries of the atom and beyond. But unfortunately, we've gotten to the point of answering questions whose motivation we've forgotten. Why should we want to live forever? Why should we want to expore new stars? Why should we even try to assure a dignified life for people we've never met? Instead of truly answering these questions, we've plastered them over with platitudes, once again moving the goalposts so that we can paint our failings as "enlightenment", as proof of our "tolerance". Anyone who doesn't see the intellectual cowardice of this is fooling themselves.
